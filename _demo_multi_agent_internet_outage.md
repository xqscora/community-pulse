I keep glancing at my phone, just to *see* it’s still there, still working. It’s almost…irritating. The way the world just feels a little less *possible* when you can’t just instantly access information. 

The biggest thing, I think, is the timing. I’ve been emailing back and forth with that robotics internship at TechForward – I really want that. They’re asking for a portfolio showcasing my project designs, and I’ve been uploading everything. It’s a huge push for me to get this internship, and the thought of being completely disconnected, unable to send those files, is… frustrating. 

My biggest worry is that this will completely derail my application. It feels like such a wasted opportunity. But honestly, I’m also hoping that this forces me to actually *build* something, offline. Maybe I can finally get serious about that autonomous drone project I’ve been sketching out – it’s a huge challenge, and the delay might actually push me to really refine my designs. I think I’ll start by spending some time at the library, researching the latest advancements in sensor technology. It feels like a chance to really focus, without all the digital noise.

## Jordan Lee — librarian

**Top drives:** curiosity=0.57, fairness=0.54, social=0.46

It was the abruptness, the absolute lack of detail. A 72-hour outage? That’s a chasm of time, especially when you’re relying on the net for everything from ordering groceries to, well, connecting with people. My dopamine spiked just a hair, a flicker of that familiar frustration – the feeling of things falling apart without a proper explanation. 

Right now, the biggest pull is curiosity, honestly. I want to understand the scale of this failure, the root cause. The social density here is already low, and this feels like it's going to amplify that. It’s a worry, I suppose, that folks who depend on online access – the elderly checking on family, the small business owner struggling to take orders – will be left even more isolated. But beyond that, there’s a quiet hope bubbling up too: a chance to disconnect, maybe. A forced slowing down. 

I’m going to start by checking with Mrs. Henderson. She runs the senior group and always worries about her members. I'll see if she's already heard whispers, and if there's anything I can do to help coordinate a little information sharing. Fairness is pulling me towards that – ensuring everyone hears about this disruption and has a chance to prepare.
